Powdery Mildew in Wheat

Powdery Mildew on wheat field

Wheat leaf showing fungal growths due to powdery mildew

Powdery Mildew colonies on wheat leaf

Type

Fungal: Erysiphe graminis

Causes

Powdery Mildew spreads through airborne spores that can infect plants under favorable conditions, typically cool and humid weather. The fungus overwinters on crop residues and can infect new crops in the next growing season.

Symptoms

  • White to grayish powdery growth on the upper surface of leaves, stems, and sometimes heads.
  • Chlorotic spots on leaves that eventually turn necrotic as the infection progresses.
  • Stunted growth and reduced tillering in severe cases.

Management

  • Resistant Varieties: Planting wheat varieties that are resistant to Powdery Mildew.
  • Fungicide Applications: Applying fungicides when the disease is first detected can help control its spread.
  • Cultural Practices: Practices such as crop rotation, proper spacing, and avoiding excessive nitrogen fertilization can reduce disease pressure.
  • Field Sanitation: Removing and destroying crop residues that can harbor the fungus.
