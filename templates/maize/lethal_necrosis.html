Lethal Necrosis Disease in Maize

Lethal Necrosis disease on maize leaf

Chlorotic mottling of entire leaf

Introduction

Maize Lethal Necrosis (MLN) is a devastating viral disease affecting maize crops. It is caused by the combination of Maize chlorotic mottle virus (MCMV) and any of several Potyviruses, such as Sugarcane mosaic virus (SCMV). MLN leads to severe yield losses and can rapidly devastate maize fields.

Causes

MLN is primarily transmitted by insect vectors such as thrips, leafhoppers, and aphids. The viruses can also be spread through contaminated seeds, tools, and mechanical means. The disease is particularly prevalent in regions with continuous maize cultivation and poor vector control.

Symptoms

  • Chlorotic mottling on leaves, starting from the base of young leaves in the whorl and extending upwards.
  • Necrosis of leaf margins, leading to a drying and death of the leaf tissues.
  • Stunted plant growth and poor ear development.
  • Premature aging and death of the plant.

Management

  • Resistant Varieties: Planting maize varieties that are resistant or tolerant to MLN.
  • Vector Control: Managing insect vectors through the use of insecticides and integrated pest management (IPM) practices.
  • Seed Health: Using certified, virus-free seeds to prevent initial infection.
  • Crop Rotation: Rotating with non-host crops to reduce the virus load in the field.
  • Field Hygiene: Removing and destroying infected plant residues and volunteer plants to minimize sources of the virus.
  • Surveillance: Regularly monitoring fields for early detection of symptoms and taking immediate action to control the spread.
