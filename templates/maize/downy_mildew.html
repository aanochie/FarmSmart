Introduction

Downy Mildew is a serious disease affecting maize, caused by several species of the fungal-like oomycete pathogen Peronosclerospora. The disease can cause significant yield losses and is prevalent in warm, humid climates.

Type

Fungal: Peronosclerospora sorghi

Variants:

  • Peronosclerospora maydis (Java downy mildew)
  • Peronosclerospora philippinensis (Philippine downy mildew)
  • Peronosclerospora sacchari (Sugarcane downy mildew)
  • Scleropthora rayssiae var. zeae (Brown stripe downy mildew)

Causes

Downy Mildew spreads through airborne spores and infected seed. The pathogen thrives in warm, moist conditions and can persist in soil and crop residues, making management challenging.

Symptoms

  • Yellow to pale green streaks on leaves.
  • White, downy fungal growth on the underside of leaves, especially during humid conditions.
  • Stunted plant growth and distorted leaves.
  • Poor ear development and reduced grain yield.

Management

  • Resistant Varieties: Planting maize varieties that are resistant to Downy Mildew.
  • Seed Treatment: Using fungicide-treated seeds to prevent initial infection.
  • Crop Rotation: Rotating with non-host crops to reduce the build-up of the pathogen in the soil.
  • Field Sanitation: Removing and destroying infected plant debris to reduce the source of inoculum.
  • Fungicide Applications: Applying fungicides can help control the disease, particularly in high-risk areas.
